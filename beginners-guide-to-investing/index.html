Key Terms Glossary

New to investing? Learn these terms and you're already ahead of most beginners.

Ticker Symbol
Short code for a company on the stock market. Apple = AAPL, Nvidia = NVDA, Tesla = TSLA.
Market Cap
Total value of a company. Share price x shares outstanding. Apple is roughly $3 trillion.
Sector
The industry a company operates in — Technology, Healthcare, Energy, Financials, Consumer Goods, etc.
ETF
Exchange-Traded Fund. A basket of many stocks in one product — instant diversification at low cost.
Index
A list of companies tracked together. The S&P 500 tracks the 500 largest US companies.
Dividend
Cash paid to shareholders from company profits — typically quarterly. A form of passive income.
Portfolio
The full collection of all your investments — ETFs, individual stocks, and other assets.
ISA
Individual Savings Account — a UK tax wrapper. All gains inside are 100% tax-free. Limit: £20,000/yr.
JISA
Junior ISA — tax-free investment account for children. Can open from birth. Limit: £9,000/yr. Matures at 18.
Bull / Bear Market
Bull = rising market (optimism). Bear = falling market (pessimism). Both are normal and always temporary.
Volatility
How much a price moves up and down. High volatility = bigger swings in both directions.
Compound Growth
Earning returns on your returns. Over long periods, this is what creates real wealth from small contributions.
Chapter One
Why Invest?
Keeping money in a savings account means inflation slowly eats away at its value. Investing puts your money to work so it grows faster than inflation.

The problem with doing nothing

If inflation averages 3% a year and your savings account pays 2%, you're effectively losing 1% of your money's real purchasing power every year. £10,000 today will buy you less in 10 years if it just sits in cash.

The stock market has historically returned an average of 7-12% per year over long periods. That's the difference between your money slowly shrinking and it multiplying many times over.

Historical stock market average returns: The S&P 500 has returned approximately 10% per year on average since 1957. Global markets average around 7-9%. Individual years vary wildly, but the long-term trend is consistently upward.

Chapter Two
Accounts & Platforms
Before you buy a single share, you need the right account. Platform fees and tax wrappers might sound boring — but over 20 years they can be worth more than your actual investment returns.

Use a Stocks and Shares ISA

In the UK, every adult gets a £20,000 annual ISA allowance. Any money inside a Stocks & Shares ISA grows completely tax-free — no capital gains tax, no dividend tax, nothing. You keep every single penny of profit.

Without an ISA, you'd pay Capital Gains Tax (up to 24%) on profits and Income Tax on dividends. Over decades of compounding, those tax savings are enormous — often hundreds of thousands of pounds over a lifetime.

ISA rules: £20,000 allowance per tax year (April to April). Only one Stocks & Shares ISA per year. Unused allowance does not roll over — use it or lose it. Money can be withdrawn at any time with no penalties.

The Junior ISA — the most powerful head start you can give a child

A Junior ISA (JISA) lets you open a tax-free investment account for a child from the day they are born, with an annual allowance of £9,000 per year. The money is locked until the child turns 18, then becomes their own ISA automatically.

What could a JISA realistically become? Investing just £150/month from birth at 9% average annual return gives roughly £82,000 by age 18. That covers university fees, a car, driving lessons, and still leaves a head start on their own investing journey — all from £150/month. The earlier you start, the more time does the work.

Chapter Three
Investing in ETFs
An ETF is the single best investment tool for most people. Simple, low-cost, and diversified by design. Most professional fund managers cannot consistently beat a plain index ETF over 20 years.

What is an ETF?

An Exchange-Traded Fund holds a collection of hundreds or thousands of stocks in one product. Buying one share of an S&P 500 ETF instantly gives you a tiny slice of every company in the S&P 500 — Apple, Nvidia, Microsoft, and hundreds more. Instant diversification at very low cost.

How much in commodities? Most long-term investors allocate 5-15% of their portfolio to gold or commodities as a hedge. It won't grow as fast as equities in bull markets — but it protects you significantly when equities fall hard.

Vanguard vs Invesco — does it matter?

Multiple ETFs often track the same index. The only thing that matters is the TER (Total Expense Ratio) — the annual fee deducted from the fund's returns. Look for below 0.20%. Both Vanguard and Invesco S&P 500 ETFs have TERs around 0.07%. The performance difference over 30 years is negligible. Pick whichever is cheaper and available on your platform.

Accumulation (Acc) vs Distribution (Dist): Accumulation ETFs automatically reinvest dividends back into the fund, growing your holding. Distribution ETFs pay dividends out as cash. For long-term wealth building, always choose Accumulation — it fully harnesses compound growth without you having to do anything.

Speculative stocks

Speculative stocks are smaller, earlier-stage companies with high growth potential — but also a realistic chance of losing most or all of their value. Examples: pre-profit companies, penny stocks, very small market caps, or businesses in unproven markets with heavy debt.

Sizing rule: Never put more than 3-5% of your total portfolio in any single speculative position. If it goes to zero — and it might — your overall portfolio barely registers it. If it 10x's, you still benefit meaningfully.

Sensible approach

70-80% ETFs, 15-20% blue chips across multiple sectors, max 3-5% in any single speculative position.

Risky approach

Concentrating everything in 2-3 speculative stocks hoping for a 10x. This is how people lose everything quickly.

Chapter Five
What Moves Markets
Prices don't move randomly. Understanding what drives markets up and down helps you stay calm when things get turbulent — and avoid expensive emotional decisions.

Key market-moving factors

📋
Quarterly Earnings
Every public company reports financial results four times per year. Beat expectations and the stock typically jumps. Miss expectations and it can fall 10-20% in a single day. Earnings season (January, April, July, October) is when individual stocks move most violently.
🏢
Sector Rotation
Large institutional investors regularly move money between sectors based on economic conditions. When interest rates rise, money tends to flow out of tech and into financials and energy. This "rotation" causes entire sectors to move up or down together regardless of individual company performance.
🌎
Geopolitical Events
Wars, trade disputes, sanctions, elections, and diplomatic tensions all move markets. Uncertainty typically causes investors to sell risky assets (stocks) and buy safe havens (gold, bonds). Supply chain disruptions from conflicts spike energy and commodity prices, affecting entire sectors.
🧠
Investor Sentiment
Markets are partly driven by emotion. Fear causes panic selling that pushes prices far below fair value. Greed causes bubbles that push prices far above it. The VIX (fear index) and CNN Fear & Greed Index measure this in real time. Extreme fear is historically often a great time to buy.
🏭
Federal Reserve (US Stocks)
The US Federal Reserve sets interest rates for the world's largest economy. When the Fed raises rates, company profits shrink and investors move money from stocks into bonds. When rates fall, stocks typically rally hard. Every Fed meeting and press conference is watched closely by the entire global market.
🏮
Bank of England (UK Stocks)
The Bank of England performs the same role for UK markets. Rate decisions directly affect FTSE 100 stocks — financials benefit from higher rates while property companies are hurt. Its decisions are critical for UK-listed companies and the value of sterling-denominated assets.
📈
Inflation Data
Monthly CPI inflation reports are among the most market-moving data releases. High inflation forces central banks to raise rates (bad for stocks). Falling inflation signals rates may drop (good for stocks). Markets often move before the decision even happens, as investors price in expected outcomes.
👔
Economic Data
Jobs reports (US non-farm payrolls), GDP growth figures, consumer spending, and manufacturing indexes all signal the economy's health. Strong data = growth = stocks tend to rise. Weak data = recession fears = stocks tend to fall. These follow fixed monthly schedules that investors plan around.
🔨
Technological Disruption
New technologies destroy entire industries while creating new ones. AI disrupted old-guard tech companies and rewarded those who adapted. EVs are disrupting traditional automakers. Awareness of long-term structural shifts helps position a portfolio for the next decade rather than the last one.

US stocks vs UK stocks

As a UK investor you can invest in UK-listed or US-listed stocks and ETFs. They behave very differently and come with different costs worth understanding.

🇺🇸
US Stocks
  • Significantly higher long-term growth — S&P 500 averages ~10%/yr historically
  • Home to the world's most innovative companies: Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft
  • Technology, AI, biotech, and high-growth sectors are US-dominated
  • Far greater market liquidity and depth
  • FX fee: platforms charge 0.15-0.5% to convert GBP to USD on each purchase
  • FX impact: if the pound strengthens vs the dollar, your GBP returns reduce even if the stock rises
  • No stamp duty on US stock purchases
🇬🇧
UK Stocks
  • No currency risk — everything priced in GBP
  • High dividend yields — UK companies return more cash to shareholders
  • No FX fee when buying
  • FTSE 100 dominated by banks, energy, and mining — mature sectors
  • Lower long-term capital growth than US markets
  • 0.5% stamp duty on individual UK stock purchases (not on ETFs)
  • Far fewer high-growth technology companies listed in the UK

FX fee vs FX impact — simply explained:

FX fee is the cost your platform charges to convert GBP into USD when you buy a US stock — typically 0.15% to 0.5% of the trade. It's a one-time transaction cost, like a currency exchange fee at the airport.

FX impact is the effect of exchange rate movement on your returns over time. Example: you buy Apple at $200 when £1 = $1.25, paying £160. Apple rises to $220, but now £1 = $1.40 (pound got stronger). Your $220 only converts back to £157 — you lost money in GBP terms despite the stock rising. This also works in your favour if the pound weakens.

Stamp Duty on UK stocks: When you buy shares in a UK-listed company (Barclays, BP, Tesco etc.), the government charges 0.5% stamp duty on the purchase. On a £2,000 investment that's £10 to HMRC immediately. This does NOT apply to ETFs — another reason many beginners prefer ETFs to individual UK stocks.
Chapter Six
Long-Term Tips
The best investors are usually the ones who do the least. Consistent, boring, automated investing beats active trading almost every single time.
⚙
Auto-invest
Set up automatic monthly investments so money moves to your ISA without you thinking about it. Trading 212 Pies does this brilliantly. Remove the decision, remove the temptation to skip months.
📅
Invest consistently, not perfectly
Trying to time the market almost never works. Investing the same amount every month — pound-cost averaging — means you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they're high.
👁
Stop checking your portfolio
Daily checking leads to emotional decisions. Markets drop 10-20% regularly — that's completely normal. Checking constantly makes normal movements feel catastrophic when they aren't. Review quarterly at most.
📊
Never withdraw in a crash
When markets fall 30-40%, every instinct says sell. But crashes are temporary and recoveries are certain for diversified portfolios. Investors who held through 2008, 2020, and 2022 all eventually hit new highs.
⇧
Increase contributions over time
As your income grows, increase what you invest — even slightly. Going from £200/month to £250/month can add tens of thousands to your final portfolio over 20 years due to compounding.
♻
Reinvest everything
Use Accumulation ETFs so dividends reinvest automatically. Never withdraw returns to spend unless you genuinely need to. Every £1 that stays invested keeps compounding year after year.
🛡
Max your ISA first
Before investing in any taxable account, fill your ISA allowance (£20,000/year). The tax savings over 20-30 years are enormous — potentially hundreds of thousands in avoided capital gains tax.
⌛
Start as early as possible
The most powerful variable is time. Starting at 20 vs 30 can mean a 2-3x larger final portfolio, even investing the same amount every month. There is no good reason to wait.
🗿
Boring is good
A plain S&P 500 ETF held for 30 years with monthly contributions will outperform the vast majority of actively managed funds and stock pickers. Boring means it's working exactly as it should.

Avoiding TikTok stocks

Social media — especially TikTok and YouTube — is full of people promoting individual stocks, hyping up "the next 100x" opportunity, or claiming to have found a stock about to explode. This content is almost always misleading, and acting on it is one of the fastest ways to lose money.

Why TikTok stock tips are dangerous: Creators only show their wins. You never see the losses — the ten stocks that went to zero before the one that went up. They thrive on FOMO (fear of missing out), creating urgency so you act before thinking. Many are paid to promote stocks, meaning they have a financial incentive to get you buying regardless of whether it's any good.
🎲
Survivorship bias
You only ever see the success stories. For every person who turned £500 into £50,000 on a speculative stock, there are hundreds who turned £500 into £0. The losers don't post videos about it. The winners post it on repeat.
🔥
FOMO and manipulation
"This stock is about to explode — get in before it's too late" is the language of manipulation, not analysis. Genuine market-moving information doesn't come through 60-second videos. It creates panic buying that benefits whoever is selling to you.
🤔
If they knew, everyone would be rich
If a creator genuinely knew a stock was about to triple, why would they tell millions of people for free? They'd invest everything they own and retire quietly. The fact they're making content about it tells you everything you need to know.

The alternative: Stick to boring investing. An S&P 500 ETF with monthly contributions, left alone for 20-30 years, will outperform the vast majority of people chasing TikTok tips. Wealth is built slowly, quietly, and consistently — not in viral videos.

Chapter Seven
Your Game Plan
A clear, step-by-step roadmap — from getting your finances in order to building a serious long-term investment portfolio. Follow this in sequence and you'll be ahead of 95% of people.

The investing roadmap

Investing works best when built on a stable financial foundation. Rushing into the stock market while carrying high-interest debt or without a budget is like pouring water into a leaking bucket. Do the steps in order.

1
Get debt-free (except your mortgage)

Before you invest a single pound, clear any high-interest debt — credit cards, car finance, personal loans. It makes no sense to earn 9% in the stock market while paying 20-25% interest on a credit card. The guaranteed return of paying off debt beats any investment return at that rate.

Your mortgage is the exception — it's typically low interest and tied to an appreciating asset. No need to clear that before you start investing.

2
Build an emergency fund

Before investing, keep 3-6 months of living expenses in an easy-access savings account. This is your buffer — it stops you being forced to sell investments at a bad time if something unexpected happens. Investments should only ever be money you genuinely don't need for years.

3
Get on a budget and find your investing amount

Track your income and expenses. Find the money you can comfortably invest every month without affecting your lifestyle. You don't need to start big — £100/month is a perfectly fine starting point. The habit matters far more than the amount in the early stages.

As your income grows, increase your contribution. Going from £100 to £200 to £300/month over a few years of career growth will have an enormous effect on your final outcome.

4
Start simple — invest in ETFs only

Open a Stocks & Shares ISA on Trading 212 and start with a simple ETF portfolio. The FTSE All-World (VWRP) or S&P 500 (VUAG/CSPX) are excellent starting points — no need for anything more complex than this for years.

Set up auto-invest and let it run. Don't check it every day. Don't try to time the market. Just keep contributing every month, consistently.

5
Gradually add small positions in blue chip stocks

Once you're comfortable with how investing works and your portfolio has grown, you can start adding small individual positions in established blue chip companies — Nvidia, Apple, JPMorgan, Eli Lilly, Coca-Cola. Keep these as a small supplement to your ETF core, not a replacement for it.

ETFs should remain at least 70-80% of your portfolio. Individual stocks are the seasoning, not the meal.

6
Only then — very small speculative positions

If you want exposure to higher-risk, higher-reward opportunities, keep each speculative position to 3-5% of your total portfolio maximum. If it goes to zero, it shouldn't meaningfully affect your overall wealth. If it rockets, you benefit without having bet the house on it.

Never let speculative positions grow large through wins without rebalancing — conviction is fine, dangerous concentration is not.

Which accounts to use — and in what order

The UK tax system gives you several tax-efficient wrappers. Use them in order of priority, always filling the most tax-efficient account first before moving to the next.

Priority 1
Your Stocks & Shares ISA — £20,000/year
Your primary investing account. Every person who invests in the UK should use this first. All gains, dividends, and interest are 100% tax-free for life. Open one on Trading 212 for free and fill this before anything else. This is where the majority of people will be investing their entire lives.
Priority 2
Spouse's Stocks & Shares ISA — another £20,000/year
If your income is high enough that you're regularly maxing your own ISA (£20,000/year), your spouse or partner also has their own £20,000 allowance. Together a couple can shelter £40,000/year from tax completely. This only makes sense once your own ISA is being consistently maxed each year.
Priority 3
Junior ISA for your children — £9,000/year per child
If you have children, the Junior ISA is one of the most powerful financial tools available. Up to £9,000/year per child, all tax-free, compounding for up to 18 years. Even modest monthly contributions can give your child a transformative financial head start — university, a car, a house deposit, or all three.
Priority 4
General Investment Account — unlimited, but taxable
Once you're maxing ISAs and JISAs and still have surplus income to invest, a General Investment Account lets you invest without limit. However, gains above your annual Capital Gains Tax allowance (currently £3,000) will be taxed. Reaching this point is a genuinely good problem to have — it means you're investing seriously and consistently.

The simple version: Start with your ISA. Invest in ETFs. Keep it boring. As your income grows, invest more. Only once you're comfortable do you add individual stocks. Only once you're well-established do you consider speculative positions. Most people only ever need steps 1-4 — and that's completely fine. The S&P 500 compounding for 30 years will make almost anyone wealthy if they start early and stay consistent.

Remember: There is no rush to reach step 5 or 6. The most important thing is to start, stay consistent, and let time do the work. Someone who invests £100/month in an S&P 500 ETF from age 22 will almost certainly end up wealthier than someone who waits until 35 and invests £500/month.
